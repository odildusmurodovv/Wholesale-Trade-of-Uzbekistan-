# Wholesale-Trade-of-Uzbekistan-
Analysis of Uzbekistan's wholesale trade turnover (2010–2024), covering national trends, nominal vs. real growth, and regional distribution, built from official State Statistics Agency (stat.uz) data.

1. Long run trend: rapid nominal growth
    National wholesale trade turnover rose from 11.8 trillion so'm (2010) to 456.6 trillion so'm (2024) - shows the nominal CAGR of 29.9% over 14 tears. However that headline number is misleading on its own: nominal growth in 2024 (18.4%) was more than 1.5x in real, inflation-adjusted growth rate(11.8%). Even though the gap has been present throughout the series it narrowed considerably - nominal growth peaked near 50% in 2019-2020 and 2023, while real growth sat closer to 25%-32% in those same years. 

2. Market structure: Extreme concentration
     Tashkent city alone accounts for 53.8% of national turnover - more than every other region combined. Thashkent region adds another 11.7%, meaning the capital region controls roughly two-thirds of all wholesale trade activity in the country. That is appropriate that it reflects Tashkent's role as the logistics and. the distribution hub for whole country. However it does mean national-level growth figures are disproportionately driven by what happens in one city.

3. Nominal vs Real growth
      Here is the most interesting pattern in the data - the largest markets are growing the slowest. Toshkent city (53.8% of turnover) grew just 9.3% in real terms in 2024 — below the national average of 11.8%. Surxondaryo and Buxoro, both small cities (3.2% and 3.9% share), grew even slower (3.2% and 5.3%). Meanwhile, mid-sized regions — Toshkent region (17.8%), Samarqand (17.3%), Farg'ona (14.4%) — are growing at nearly double the national rate.

Jizzax breaks this pattern entirely: despite holding only 1.2% of national turnover, it grew 50.5% in real terms — more than 4x the national average and 2.8x the next-fastest region. That's large enough to warrant a specific caveat rather than just a celebratory headline: with a small base (5.7 trillion so'm), a single large infrastructure or wholesale project coming online could produce a 50% swing that a similarly-sized absolute change wouldn't register at all in Tashkent. Before treating Jizzax as "the growth story," it's worth checking whether this is broad-based growth or one-off.

4. Comparison between 2025 and 2026
      Comparing the same five months (Jan–May) year over year: 2026's cumulative growth index is running at 15.4% vs 9.8% for the same period in 2025 — a meaningfully stronger start. But the shape differs too: 2025 dipped and rebounded (110.6 → 112.3 → 110.7 → 110.1 → 109.8, essentially flat-to-declining across the period), while 2026 shows more volatility with a higher peak (115.6 → 116.5 → 110.9 → 112.7 → 115.4). The higher average with more month-to-month swing suggests 2026 growth may be less steady/more event-driven than 2025's, though five months of cumulative data isn't enough to confirm that — this is a hypothesis to revisit once more months are in.

5. Summary
     The topline "456.6 trillion so'm, +18.4%" is technically accurate but tells an incomplete story on its own. The fuller picture: growth is real but overstated by price effects (though less so than in prior years); it's heavily concentrated in one region; the fastest expansion is happening in smaller, previously under-weighted regions rather than the dominant hub; and one region (Jizzax) is growing at a rate extreme enough to deserve investigation into its cause before being cited as a trend.
